Characterization

Know what is in your pipelines: continuously, accurately, and without stopping to find out. Real-time measurement of the parameters that drive operational decisions.

The Problem

What Happens When You Can't See Your Process

Mineral processing plants move complex slurry through long pipelines, and the parameters that govern efficiency, safety, and compliance can change continuously. Historically, available instruments have often struggled in the process environment or have not delivered the slurry-specific visibility operators need.

The following are common examples. They are illustrative, not an exhaustive inventory of every measurement gap.

Over-Grinding and Reagent Waste

Without continuous feed characterization, grinding circuits default to conservative set points. Over-grinding consumes excess energy and reagents. Every tonne processed without accurate, real-time density data is a tonne processed at suboptimal conditions.

Pipe Wear With No Warning

Slurry is abrasive. Pipe wall thinning progresses continuously in high-velocity lines. Without ongoing wall thickness measurement, the first sign of a problem is often a breach: unplanned downtime, environmental incident, and an emergency repair on your timeline, not yours.

Compliance Data From Spot Samples

Environmental reporting often relies on periodic grab samples, which capture only isolated moments in a continuously changing process and can leave gaps in what operators can see and report.

The Instrumentation Gap

Why Existing Instruments Struggle in Slurry

Mineral processing slurry creates measurement conditions that are difficult for conventional instruments, including abrasion, opacity, and changing flow behaviour.

☢

Nuclear / Radiometric Density Gauges

A widely used incumbent technology for slurry density measurement. They require radioactive source licensing, radiation safety program management, periodic regulatory reporting, and eventual disposal of spent sources. They provide a single measurement across the pipe, with no spatial resolution. Source decay can require periodic recalibration and, over time, source replacement.

⚙

Intrusive Magnetic Flowmeters

Require pipe penetration and wetted electrodes in direct contact with the slurry. In abrasive slurry service, the liner and electrodes can wear over time, increasing maintenance needs and sometimes causing unplanned process interruptions for repair.

🧪

Manual Grab Sampling

Widely used for slurry characterization, but it captures only discrete points in time. Sampling is often hourly or less frequent, which adds latency, labour cost, and analyst variability while leaving process excursions between samples unseen.

📏

Differential Pressure and Coriolis Meters

Differential pressure meters require flow restrictions in the pipe, which can be vulnerable to scaling and blockage in mineral slurry service. Coriolis meters require the full process flow to pass through a vibrating element, and wear or plugging can limit their practicality in slurry applications with higher solids content.

〰

Non-Intrusive Flow Meters

Clamp-on instruments offer a major step forward by measuring bulk flow velocity without pipe penetration or wetted parts. Their scope is still limited, though: they can measure flow and, in some cases, void fraction, but not the full slurry picture on their own. For operations that want to replace the nuclear gauge or understand the process more completely, important gaps can remain.

🧬

Rheological Properties

Inline rheological measurement is available, but existing solutions involve trade-offs. Bypass systems divert part of the process stream and add delay. Direct-contact probes measure viscosity at a point and must operate in abrasive slurry conditions. A fully non-intrusive, continuous view of the flow curve from outside the pipe remains an important gap.

🔩

Equipment Health

Conventional process instrumentation shows what is flowing, but changes in flow can also reveal developing equipment problems earlier than typical monitoring methods. By capturing those changes continuously, flow characterization can provide an earlier signal that something in the equipment is starting to degrade.
